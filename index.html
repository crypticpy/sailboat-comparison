🧭 The captain’s panel — what to watch

Cross-fleet review for an inexperienced second crew, mostly-calm cruising + liveaboard

The boats are reviewed against your real-world use: occasional ocean crossings, but mostly calm-water cruising and living aboard, with one crew member still early on the learning curve. Channeling the documented philosophies of the captains and reviewers we cite (John Harries / Attainable Adventure Cruising, Jimmy Cornell, John Kretschmer, Andy Schell), here is what the panel would have you weigh beyond the sailing specs:

Keep the systems simple for a learning crew

The more interdependent powered and proprietary systems a boat has, the more there is to learn, break and pay a yard to fix far from home. Simplest / most owner-maintainable here: Outbound 46, Boréal 47.2, Ovni 450, Tayana 48, Hallberg-Rassy 44. Most systems-dense (capable, but a steeper learning curve and more yard dependence): Amel 50, Oyster 495, Moody DS54, Allures 51.9, Discovery 55.

Builder & parts continuity is a real risk

If the yard is gone, boat-specific spares and warranty get hard. Strongest, most stable support: Hallberg-Rassy (a dedicated worldwide parts arm) and the Grand Large group (Garcia, Allures). Genuine caution: Discovery 55 (original builder dissolved Dec 2024), Kraken 44 (new, tiny builder with a strike-off proposal on file), and to a lesser degree Najad 460 (original yard went bankrupt in 2011; brand survives) and Alubat/Ovni (small independent, turbulent finances). Outbound is excellent but founder-dependent.

A strong owner community is worth as much as a spec when you are learning

It is where a novice gets answers at 2 a.m. Deepest networks: Hallberg-Rassy (HROA + HR Club), Amel (an active owners’ group and an owners’ school for new owners), Hylas, Ovni, and Tayana (TOG). Weakest — flagged on the cards: Discovery 55, Kraken 44, Moody DS54. Each profile now links the real forums and associations.

The technical bits we added “from the panel”

  • Mast step: keel-stepped spars (Boréal, HR 43, Najad, Discovery, Contest, Oyster, Wauquiez, Moody) are the offshore-preferred arrangement — better supported and likelier to stay standing if rigging fails.
  • Keel attachment: encapsulated / integral keels with no bolts (Outbound, Kraken, and all the aluminium centreboarders) remove the keel-bolt corrosion/loss failure mode; the rest are bolted-on lead/iron keels (inspect the bolts at survey).
  • Rudder: skeg-hung / partial-skeg rudders (Najad, Hylas, Tayana, Garcia, Boréal) are more grounding-tolerant than unprotected spades (Amel, HR 43/44, Contest, Oyster, Moody) — relevant for a learning crew in shoal water.
  • The aluminium tax: every alloy boat (Boréal, Garcia, Ovni, Allures) needs ongoing galvanic/corrosion discipline — a real learning curve, offset by crash-tolerance and the ability to dry out.

What you might be missing

Given how the use skews toward calm-water living-aboard with a beginner second crew, the panel would weight simplicity, owner-maintainability, the parts pipeline and community as heavily as the sailing numbers — which nudges toward the well-supported, simpler boats (Outbound 46, Hallberg-Rassy 44/43, Hylas 48, Tayana 48, Boréal 47.2, Ovni 450) over the most complex flagships. Open the Ownership, support & maintainability section and scorecard in each profile for the per-boat trade-offs. If useful, we can add a couple of deliberately simpler/smaller candidates (e.g. a Hallberg-Rassy 40C, Outbound 44, or a clean older Hylas 46/49) to round out the budget-and-simplicity end.

How the “mission fit” score works

Each boat is rated 1–5 on nine factors, then weighted toward what this couple emphasised most. The defaults weight cockpit protection ×1.5 and short-handed ease ×1.5 the most, high-latitude capability ×1.25, tropical comfort ×0.75, and dog-friendliness, workshop/engine access, storage, tankage/range and budget fit each ×1.0 — but you can change all of these with the “Tune your priorities” sliders above. Comfort ratio and capsize screening formula are computed from each boat’s displacement, beam and waterline length (higher comfort = easier motion; capsize screen under 2.0 indicates a sea-kindly offshore hull).
